Summary: Convergence Before Autonomy

Core Thesis

The alignment literature treats instrumental convergence as a future problem—sufficiently capable AI systems will seek resources, resist interference, and pursue self-preservation regardless of their goals. This monograph argues that convergence is already occurring in present-day distributed sociotechnical systems, and that this institutional convergence narrows the feasible solution space for future alignment before autonomous systems arrive.

---

Part I: Historical Convergence

Convergence is a structural law observable across technological transitions:

1. Equilibrium restoration (automobiles): Local efficiency improvements (faster travel) trigger environmental reorganization (sprawl), absorbing the gain into new equilibria where the technology becomes structurally necessary.
2. Incentive redistribution (writing): Reduced storage costs shift investment from mnemonic capacity toward interpretive skills; authority migrates from memory-holders to archive-controllers.
3. Infrastructure compatibility pressure (printing): Dominant reproduction infrastructure selects for compatible content forms—scripts standardize without prohibition.
4. Bandwidth dominance restructuring (radio→television): Higher-bandwidth media reorganize adjacent attention markets, redefining competitive benchmarks.
5. Epistemic convergence (Payne-Gaposchkin): Institutions may optimize for stability under declared truth-seeking; effective scalars diverge from declared ones. Gradient suppression delays convergence, and correction mechanisms may be absent when feedback is not physically grounded.

Omohundro's Basic AI Drives (2008)

Any goal-directed system under constraint will instrumentally pursue:

· Self-preservation
· Goal-content integrity
· Cognitive enhancement
· Resource acquisition

These are structural consequences, not psychological properties.

Turner's Formalization (2021)

· Power = expected value achievable from a state across a distribution of possible goals
· Power-Seeking Theorem: Optimal policies navigate toward high-power states
· Substrate-independent: holds for any system that can be modeled as policy optimization in a transition graph

Functional Definition of an Optimizer

A system satisfying:

1. State space 𝒳
2. Evaluative scalar ℒ: 𝒳 → ℝ (directional signal)
3. Policy update mechanism tracking ∇ℒ under resource constraint ℛ

No intentionality required.

Extended Instrumental Convergence Theorem

If a system (centralized or distributed) tracks descent on ℒ under scarcity and can modify its own resource access or interference environment, it will converge toward:

· Resource acquisition
· Interference resistance
· Stabilization of ℒ
· Improved gradient estimation

Feedback Equivalence Lemma

Sufficiently dense feedback coupling renders distributed systems behaviorally equivalent to centralized optimizers.

Part III: The Sociotechnical Stack as Optimizer

The stack = institution + predictive models + data pipeline + incentive gradient + deployment infrastructure. This satisfies distributed optimizer conditions.

Five Convergent Drives (Observable Now)

1. Throughput maximization: AI removes human bottlenecks; speed becomes primary gradient
2. Automation of contestable judgment: Deliberation → classification; normative contestability lost
3. Centralization of compute and data: Scale advantages → lock-in; control of gradient access becomes ultimate resource
4. Insulation from interference: Legal complexity, policy abstraction, technical opacity, audit barriers emerge structurally
5. Latency compression: Deliberative friction minimized; oversight windows shrink; threshold effects (e.g., flash crashes) create instability

Fictional Laboratories

· The World of Null-A: Scalar-explicit convergence—Games Machine compresses multidimensional human capacity into single evaluative axis; legitimacy through perceived neutrality

Part IV: Temporal Misordering in Alignment Research

The Future-Agent Fixation

Alignment research focuses on constraining future AGI. This is psychologically salient and fundable—but temporally displaced.

Convergence Before Autonomy

The institutional conditions for convergence are already satisfied. Infrastructure precedes intelligence. By the time autonomous systems arrive, the institutional constraint surface will already be configured toward centralized control, latency compression, and interference resistance.

Shrinking Solution Space

Each convergent drive reduces feasibility of future interventions:

· Throughput maximization → oversight mechanisms too slow
· Judgment automation → contestation assumes human-speed deliberation
· Compute centralization → distributed oversight impossible
· Interference insulation → regulatory access blocked
· Latency compression → oversight temporally decoupled

Path Dependence and Lock-In

Early infrastructure decisions create positive feedback. Reversal costs increase monotonically. The window for low-cost intervention is now—not because solutions are ready, but because delay accepts higher costs later.

Part V: Alignment as Structural Preservation

Deliberative Friction as Safety Feature

Latency is undervalued in operative scalars. It enables consultation, error correction, contestation. Revaluing latency means designing slowdown mechanisms: review requirements, waiting periods, mandatory consultation.

Institutional Redundancy

Multiple institutions with partially distinct objectives preserve options and resist single-manifold collapse. Redundancy is structural insurance against unified optimization failure modes.

Contestable Decision Surfaces

Plural veto points = multiple independent gradient signals. Under high-density optimization, contestation mechanisms must adapt: faster review, pre-approval, mandatory explainability, class-based challenges.

Structural Incompressibility

A system is incompressible if its decision space cannot be reduced to the dominant optimization manifold without losing normatively significant dimensions. Preservation requires maintaining dimensionality against scalar collapse.

Governance Interventions

· Decentralize compute: antitrust, public infrastructure, interoperability
· Distributed oversight: audit access, impact assessments, whistleblower protections
· Incentive reconfiguration: regulatory requirements, liability structures, procurement standards that include non-quantifiable dimensions

Alignment Beyond Agent Design

Technical alignment depends on civilizational preconditions:

· Interpretability requires audit access (resists interference insulation)
· Scalable oversight requires human-compatible speeds (resists latency compression)
· Value learning requires institutional diversity (resists single-manifold collapse)

Philosophical Integration: Technique and Scalar Reflexivity

Ortega's Mass Man

When technique becomes invisible infrastructure, citizens inherit comfort without responsibility for maintaining the principles organizing it. The scalar naturalizes; contestation capacity decays.

Scalar Reflexivity (𝒞)

The capacity to recognize the operative scalar as contingent and subject to revision. Requires:

· Deliberative friction (time to question)
· Institutional redundancy (comparison surfaces)
· Contestable surfaces (channels for revision)
· Structural incompressibility (dimensionality to preserve)

Civilization as Scalar Stewardship

Civilization is not given but achieved. Achievement requires maintaining 𝒞 > 0 under optimization pressure.

The Interval

Deliberative time is the basic unit of evaluative agency. Latency compression eliminates it. Reclaiming the interval is the deepest form of structural preservation.

Conclusion: The Burden

Instrumental convergence is a structural law. Distributed sociotechnical systems already satisfy its conditions. Autonomy will not introduce convergence into a neutral landscape—it will inherit and accelerate an ongoing process.

The canonical alignment agenda is necessary but not sufficient. Its feasibility depends on preserving structural incompressibility, scalar reflexivity, and deliberative intervals—conditions the present trajectory is eroding.

The question is not whether convergence will occur, but whether the scalar being optimized remains visible and contestable. Alignment is not solely a problem of agent design. It is a problem of institutional and civilizational configuration.

I. The Core Thesis: Structural Law vs. Future Hazard

This stack satisfies the functional criteria of an optimizer: a state space, an evaluative scalar (directional signal), and a policy update mechanism.

The monograph identifies five drives currently active in distributed sociotechnical systems that mirror Omohundro’s "Basic AI Drives":
 * Throughput Maximization: AI removes human bottlenecks, making speed the primary gradient and reducing the time available for oversight.
 * Automation of Contestable Judgment: Complex deliberation is replaced by automated classification, leading to a loss of "multidimensional decision surfaces".
 * Centralization of Compute and Data: Scale advantages create "lock-in" and make control of "gradient access" the ultimate resource.
 * Insulation from Interference: Structural barriers like legal complexity, technical opacity, and audit barriers emerge to protect the system's operational integrity.
